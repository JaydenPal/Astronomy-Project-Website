    Question Three


    Determine whether your moons are regular/native or irregular/captured.
    • (a) Are either or both of your moons likely to be regular/native moons to your planet? How do you know?/
    • (b) Are either of your moons more likely to be irregular/captured moons? How do you know?

    Regular moons orbit close to the host planet, follow nearly circular paths, and move in the same plane as the planet’s equator. In contrast, irregular moons have highly inclined, eccentric, long, and sometimes retrograde period orbits around their planet. Their orbits are highly elliptical and inclined relative to the plane of their host planet’s equator.

    i. Our first, larger moon, seems to be a native moon. Its orbital inclination is only .1 degrees and has a semi-similar density to the planet. This could mean they are made up of similar materials and would support it being a native moon. However, the most compelling evidence of it being a native moon is its low orbital eccentricity and inclination.

    i. Our second moon is a captured moon. Its orbital eccentric is incredibly high, a sign of a captured moon, and also orbits our planet at a great distance. These two pieces of evidence, as well as its lower mass and density, draws the conclusion that this moon was captured.
